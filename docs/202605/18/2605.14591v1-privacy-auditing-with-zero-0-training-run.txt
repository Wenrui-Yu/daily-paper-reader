## Abstract 

Privacy auditing provides empirical lower bounds on the differential privacy pa-rameters of learning algorithms. Existing methods, however, require interventional access to the training pipeline, either to retrain multiple times or to randomize data inclusion. This is often infeasible for large deployed systems such as foundation models. We introduce Zero-Run privacy auditing, a post-hoc framework for audit-ing models using two fixed datasets: examples known to be training-set members and examples known to be non-members. In this observational regime, membership is no longer randomized; instead, member and non-member data often differ in dis-tribution, so membership inference scores may reflect a distribution shift rather than algorithmic leakage. Drawing on ideas from causal inference, we formalize this confounding effect and propose two complementary corrections that yield valid pri-vacy audits. Our first approach models the combined effect of distribution shift and algorithmic leakage as an adaptive composition, producing conservative global cor-rections. Our second approach conditions on observed data and adjusts pointwise membership guesses, yielding sharper instance-dependent bounds. Experiments on synthetic data and large-scale models show that Zero-Run auditing enables practical privacy evaluation when retraining or controlled data insertion is infeasible. 

## 1 Introduction 

As AI systems increasingly process sensitive data, mitigating privacy leakage (i.e., the inadvertent disclosure of training examples) has become a critical priority. This risk is exacerbated by high-capacity foundation models trained across various modalities [ 39 , 21 , 32 , 7, 18 , 8]. Trained on massive, web-crawled corpora, they are inherently susceptible to memorizing personal or proprietary information [ 3, 2, 24 ], and Carlini et al. [4] show that this memorization scales log-linearly with model size. Moreover, these vulnerabilities introduce challenges in domain-specific contexts; for instance, clinical foundation models memorize rare patient profiles despite rigorous data de-identification [ 38 ]. A countermeasure to these risks is differential privacy (DP) [ 13 ], the de facto standard for privacy-preserving algorithms. By bounding the impact of any individual‚Äôs data on the result, DP protects against privacy attacks that infer sensitive information from released outputs. However, despite offering worst-case guarantees over the privacy leakage, its adoption in large-scale training pipelines is limited due to steep utility penalties [ 20 ] and the prohibitive cost of overhauling existing data-processing infrastructure [35]. Motivated by these practical barriers, privacy auditing offers an empirical complement to DP by lower-bounding an algorithm‚Äôs privacy parameters [ 9 , 19 , 30 , 5]. By probing a model‚Äôs behavior

In contrast to worst-case theoretical upper bounds, auditing quantifies empirical leakage for a specific model architecture, dataset, and threat model, yielding deployment-relevant evidence for privacy claims, compliance, and risk assessment [15]. While traditional privacy auditing requires repeatedly retraining models on randomized datasets [ 19 , 30 , 5], recent advances condense this requirement to a single randomized training run [ 37 , 25 ]. Yet, even this approach fails to scale to modern AI models. Their training consumes thousands of GPU days and relies on rigid, distributed data pipelines. Large language models perfectly capture this dilemma. Despite being trained on vast web corpora loaded with sensitive data, their immense scale, opaque data mixtures, and blackbox deployments render privacy auditing nearly impossible. Consequently, researchers and regulators lack tools to verify privacy guarantees, relying on trust and the voluntary cooperation of model providers. This creates an accountability gap: the systems posing the greatest privacy risks are the most insulated from independent privacy evaluation. 

Contributions. To address this gap, we introduce a Zero-Run auditing framework that enables auditors to assess the privacy leakage of an algorithm post-hoc from its release , without modifying the algorithm or data pipeline. We ground our approach in a fundamental analogy to causal inference. Ex-isting privacy audits based on randomized data insertion are inherently interventional‚ÄĒanalogous to randomized controlled trials‚ÄĒwhich avoid confounding by design. In contrast, our Zero-Run frame-work is the observational counterpart, estimating privacy leakage from static member and non-member datasets. Since these datasets often follow different underlying distributions, this distribution shift acts as a confounding factor [ 33 , 42 ]. Guided by this analogy, our main contributions are as follows: 1. Formalizing Zero-Run Auditing (Section 3). We formalize the post-hoc auditing regime where the auditor relies on disjoint datasets of known members and non-members. We demonstrate that naive audits on such data can substantially overestimate privacy leakage: membership inference attacks can succeed merely by exploiting distributional shift rather than algorithmic leakage, causing standard procedures to wrongly attribute this spurious signal to the model. We explicitly connect this setting to observational causal inference, where valid correction requires overlap (positivity) between the member and non-member distributions. 2. Privacy Leakage Correction (Sections 4 and 5). We extend One-Run analyses [ 37 , 25 ] to the post-hoc regime, correcting distribution shift effects on empirical privacy curves via: ‚ÄĘ The Composition Viewpoint (Section 4) models the observed leakage as an adaptive composi-tion of a distribution-shift mechanism and the audited algorithm, yielding a deconvolution-based estimate of privacy leakage, albeit a conservative one under severe distribution shift. ‚ÄĘ The Conditional Viewpoint (Section 5) characterizes pointwise privacy leakage conditional on the example. We tamper an adversary‚Äôs membership guesses to remove overconfidence induced by confounding, yielding a robust, shift-dependent correction. 3. Propensity Scores and Uncertainty Quantification (Section 6). Our composition and conditional correction principles rely on propensity scores (the probability that an example is a member, based solely on its features), which must be estimated. We explicitly quantify uncertainty in propensity score estimation in our privacy lower bounds via bootstrapping [14]. 4. Practical Scalability (Section 7). We demonstrate that Zero-Run auditing enables scalable privacy audits of large, deployed models where retraining is computationally or logistically infeasible. 

## 2 Background on Differential Privacy and Privacy Auditing 

Differential Privacy. Informally, Differential Privacy (DP) [ 13 , 12 ] ensures that a randomized algorithm‚Äôs output remains largely unchanged whether a single individual‚Äôs data is included or not in the input. DP is widely considered the standard for privacy-preserving data analysis because it provides a mathematically rigorous guarantee against adversaries with arbitrary side information. DP is immune to post-processing (protections cannot be degraded by further computation) and composes gracefully, enabling accurate tracking of the cumulative privacy loss across multiple analyses.

Privacy Auditing. An auditing algorithm takes as input an algorithm A (e.g., DP-SGD) and a privacy hypothesis H (e.g., ‚Äú A satisfies f -DP‚ÄĚ), outputting a bit indicating whether A satisfies H.Standard auditing follows a three-step procedure: (1) a randomized experiment evaluating A on a dataset D; (2) a membership inference attack (MIA) outputting guesses (Ti)i‚ąą[m] ‚ąą {‚ąí 1, 0, 1}m

p ‚ąą [0 , 1] , the audit is p-valid if P (b = FALSE |A satisfies H) ‚Č§ p. In step (1) , both Multi-Run [ 19 ] and One-Run [37 ] auditing randomize data inclusions‚ÄĒexecuting 

A repeatedly for individual points Xi, or once for multiple points (Xi)i‚ąą[m], respectively. This randomized assignment is the core statistical assumption validating interventional audits, an assumption that our Zero-Run auditing framework explicitly eliminates. 

## 3 Zero-Run Privacy Auditing Framework 

Traditional privacy auditing techniques rely on randomized experiments (see Section 2) that demand computationally prohibitive re-executions and randomized data insertions, making them infeasible for complex, large-scale systems. We address this bottleneck by replacing the interventional experiment with an observational study . Instead of randomizing input data and re-running the algorithm A, our 

Zero-Run auditor passively evaluates its existing output. This approach requires zero control over or access to the original execution pipeline.

Assumption 1 (Data Generation) . Datasets D1 = {Xi : Si = 1 } and D0 = {Xi : Si = ‚ąí1}, rep-resenting members and non-members respectively, consist of independent samples drawn from distributions P1 and P0, of identical sizes n1 = n0.There are several strategies for sourcing members and non-members, depending on the use case. 

Member dataset D1. This dataset can be any subset of the training set of the audited model, such as a random subsample or a curated subset selected to maximize potential privacy leakage. Even when the complete primary dataset is unknown to the auditor, it is often possible to identify some 

included examples. For instance, when auditing large-scale models, D1 may consist of public records, historical archives, or corpora published prior to the algorithm‚Äôs execution phase [34, 27, 28]. 

Non-member dataset D0. The most straightforward scenario occurs when a randomized test split is available, yielding a dataset D0 ‚äā X \ D train with zero distribution shift. When such a split is unavailable, alternative construction strategies are necessary. Following empirical evaluations for large language models [ 28 ], one approach is to utilize data generated after the algorithm‚Äôs execution timestamp, ensuring D0 is strictly excluded; however, a distribution shift ( P1Őł = P0) is naturally 4expected here due to temporal changes. Alternatively, one can synthesize non-members from a continuous approximation of the member distribution by training a generative model on D1 and sampling from it, yielding non-members with a close distributional match ( P0 ‚Čą P1), as seen in PANORAMIA [23]. Our framework seamlessly integrates these varied choices of D0.

Remark 1. We emphasize that, as in [ 37 , 25 ], our meta-algorithm does not restrict the auditor to algorithms that formally satisfy DP. Because our framework treats A as a black box and evaluates privacy leakage purely based on its outputs and observational data, it can be applied to arbitrary algorithms, regardless of whether they were designed with formal privacy guarantees.

## 8 Related Work 

Interventional Privacy Auditing. Ding et al. [9] , Jagielski et al. [19] introduced privacy auditing methods that rely on repeatedly running the audited algorithm with and without a specific data point. These Multi-Run techniques were later refined to obtain tighter guarantees across various settings [ 30 , 5 ], see Annamalai et al. [1] for an overview. To reduce the computational cost of rerunning the audited algorithm, Steinke et al. [37] proposed a One-Run auditing method for őĶ-DP and (őĶ, őī )-DP, later extended to f -DP by Mahloujifar et al. [25] . While our proof techniques draw on these foundational works, their validity inherently depends on randomized membership assignments controlled by the auditor. This interventional requirement motivates the development of Zero-Run 

auditing methods that can estimate post hoc privacy leakage from a deployed model. 

Observational Privacy Auditing. Kalemaj et al. [22] also audits without randomized insertion, but targets the weaker label-DP, assumes identical member and non-member feature distributions, and cannot correct for general feature-level shifts. More closely related is P ANORAMIA [23 ], which audits 

(őĶ, őī )-DP using non-members drawn from a generative model. P ANORAMIA acts as a special case of our composition framework, which we strictly generalize in two ways: we support GDP auditing and employ milder overlap assumptions (Assumption 2) by permitting an approximation error őīDS > 0.When őīDS = 0 , this recovers P ANORAMIA ‚Äôs c-closeness assumption where c = log((1 ‚ąí ő∑)/ő∑ ).Furthermore, rather than learning a full generative model, our approach reduces the correction problem to estimating the propensity score function, i.e., a probabilistic binary classifier. 

Causal Inference and Distribution Shift. Even et al. [16] evaluate membership inference attacks under distribution shift using causal principles, primarily by reweighting global attack metrics (e.g., AUC). Unlike causal reweighting approaches [ 16 ] or generative approximations [ 23 ], we do not attempt to construct non-members that approximately follow the distribution of members. Instead, we capture marginal distribution-shift leakage conditional on observations, which does not require a global overlap assumption to be valid. Our audit only requires that Assumption 1 hold, and implicitly relies on local overlaps between members and non-members rather than a global one. As explicited in Proposition 3, valid Zero-Run auditing is impossible without any local overlap (disjoint support). 

## 9 Limitations & Conclusion 

Propensity Estimation Limitation. Our zero-run framework relies on being able to properly estimate the propensity score. For natural, high-dimensional data, even under an IID assumption, this is challenging due to finite-sample estimation error, as illustrated in Figure 2(c): although the oracle propensity score is 0.5, the estimated scores deviate from this value and induces additional penalization. Finding better, more reliable ways to estimate propensity scores, although orthogonal to our work, will directly benefit our framework and the tightness of our audits. 

One-Run & MIA Limitation. While our framework successfully enables post-hoc privacy evaluation, it naturally inherits the limitations of existing One-Run algorithms. First, as observed by the baseline gap in Figure 2, Zero-Run auditing is limited by the finite-sample inefficiency intrinsic to all One-Run methods, meaning empirical bounds will inherently sit somewhat below the theoretical limit [ 37 , 25 ]. Again, any improvement in this line of work would directly benefit our framework. Second, an audit‚Äôs success is deeply tied to the baseline strength of the underlying MIA. In heavily restricted black-box deployments, particularly against DP models [ 30 ], relying purely on loss-based attacks, the raw attack signal is often too weak to extract meaningful bounds, independent of our shift corrections. 

Auditing and falsifiability of propensity score models. Our observational audit relies on estimated bounds for the propensity scores. Although these bounds cannot be formally certified, they can be empirically falsified: one can treat them as a hypothesis and audit them using Theorem C.1, 9analogously to a DP claim, rejecting the propensity model when the observed evidence contradicts the claimed bounds. This test is necessarily one-sided: passing it does not prove the bounds correct, but only indicates that no violation was found with the available data and auditing power. This motivates releasing the propensity score bounds, audit dataset, and auditing procedure together with the final audit result. A skeptic can then challenge the claimed correction for distribution shift by constructing a stronger MIA that exploits the shift beyond what the released bounds allow. Thus, while the propensity model remains unverifiable in a formal sense, openness and adversarial testing make the audit computationally falsifiable and more trustworthy in practice. The main lesson is that post-hoc privacy auditing is possible, but only as an observational inference problem. Distribution shift cannot be ignored: it is itself a source of membership information. Propensity scores provide the right language for quantifying this source, and our results show how to convert propensity bounds into valid empirical lower bounds on algorithmic privacy leakage.
