# Online Matrix Factorization, Online Private Query Release, and Online Discrepancy Minimization 

## Aleksandar Nikolov ∗ Haohua Tang † Jonathan Ullman ‡

Abstract 

In this paper we consider several related online computation problems. First, we study answering sequences of statistical queries arriving online, and being answered immediately when they arrive with differential privacy. Known matrix factorization mechanisms can answer a set of statistical queries with error bounded by the γ2 norm of their query matrix, but require that all queries are known in advance. We show that nearly the same error bounds can be achieved in the online setting for non-adaptively chosen queries. To do so, we give an online factorization algorithm that competitively matches the best offline factorization up to logarithmic factors. In the online matrix factorization problem, a new row qt of a matrix arrives at each time step t, and the algorithm needs to maintain a factorization LtRt = Qt such that at each time it appends some rows to Rt, and outputs a new row ℓt s.t. ℓtRt = qt. Our algorithm maintains the competitiveness over this online process, even if the number of rows to arrive is unknown. As another application, we give an online discrepancy minimization algorithm that achieves discrepancy competitive against the γ2 norm (and also against hereditary discrepancy) up to logarithmic factors.

21 Introduction 

Suppose that a data analyst A is analyzing a dataset x that contains potentially sensitive information about 

n individuals, and we want to enable the analyst to extract useful information about x without compromising the privacy of the individuals. Differential privacy [ DMNS06 ] has become the de facto standard way to approach this problem. On a high level, differential privacy stipulates that the analyst can only learn information about x through an algorithm M , and M must satisfy the property that changing any single data point in x has only a small effect on the distribution of outputs of M . One option for the analyst is to specify all the queries they want to ask about x ahead of time, and send them to M , which responds with its answers. We call this the offline, or batch, or non-interactive setting. The offline setting certainly has some advantages. In particular, it allows M to adapt how it computes its private answers to the structure of the queries of A, giving more accurate answers to easier sets of queries. On the other hand, specifying all queries ahead of time can be a challenging task for the analyst, who might instead prefer to explore x

gradually over time, perhaps by engaging with M through an interactive process. Can the private algorithm 

M still adapt to the structure of A’s queries in this interactive setting, and provide more accurate answers to easier queries? This question is the starting point of this paper. 

## 1.1 Private Query Release 

Let us introduce some notation and definitions so that we can discuss the question above more precisely. We model the dataset x as a collection ( x1, . . . , x n) of n elements from some data universe U . For our results, we will need the data universe to be finite, and we will denote its size by N . We think of each data point xi as holding information about one individual. We say two datasets x and x′ are neighboring, written x ∼ x′, if they differ in exactly one data point. Then differential privacy is defined as follows.

Let us note here that, also in the large dataset setting, the first results that showed factorization mechanisms achieve optimal error for offline queries also used the connection between hereditary discrepancy and differential privacy [ NTZ16 ]. In the large dataset setting, the relevant discrepancy quantity of interest is hdisc( Q), which is the maximum of hdisc( Q, n ) over all n. It turns out that the γ2 norm and hereditary discrepancy are closely related, and, in fact, γ2(Q)and hdisc (Q) are equal up to logarithmic (in m) factors for all m × N matrices Q [ MNT20 ]. Similarly to hdisc (Q, n ), one can define a matrix factorization quantity γ2(Q, n ) as the maximum γ2 norm of any submatrix of Q with n columns. Thus, we could equally well state our results in terms of γ2(Q, n ), which would be superficially more similar to the large dataset case. However, the techniques we use in the small dataset case are more closely related to hereditary discrepancy than factorization so we state these results in terms of hereditary discrepancy. 

A Remark on ℓ∞ vs. ℓ2 Error. Our results exhibit a gap in competitive ratio, where the error of our mechanism does not match the best offline lower bound. We remark that, our techniques naturally give a guarantee on the ℓ∞ error, as opposed to the weaker ℓ2 error guarantee. In fact, if we insist on ℓ∞ error, then we don’t know any algorithm that approaches the discrepancy lower bound, even in the easier offline setting. Thus, if we want a better competitive guarantee in the online setting, we would either need to improve the best offline mechanisms for ℓ∞ error or would need to find a different set of techniques that allow us to benefit from the weaker ℓ2 guarantee. 

## 1.2 Online Discrepancy Minimization 

Given the close connections between private query release, factorization norms, and hereditary discrepancy, one can reasonably ask if our techniques have implications for discrepancy theory as well. The most basic notion in discrepancy theory is the discrepancy of an m × N matrix A, defined by disc (A) = min x∈{− 1,+1 }N ∥Ax ∥∞.The hereditary discrepancy hdisc (A), introduced in the previous subsection, and equal to the maximum discrepancy of any submatrix of A, is a more robust notion that is often better behaved. As alluded to above, the γ2 norm and hereditary discrepancy of any matrix are equal up to logarithmic factors: Matouˇ sek, Nikolov, and Talwar [MNT20] showed that for any m × N matrix A,

γ2(A) = O(log m) · hdisc( A); hdisc( A) = O(plog m) · γ2(A).

Moreover, Bansal, Dadush, Garg, and Lovett [ BDGL19 ] showed that there is a polynomial time algorithm that, on input the matrix A, computes an x ∈ {− 1, +1 }N which achieves ∥Ax ∥∞ = O(√log m) · γ2(A). A natural question, analogous to the questions we study in online private query release, is whether such an x

can also be computed online, when the matrix A is revealed gradually. It turns out that our techniques allow us to do so, as we explain in more detail below. ## 1.3 Online Factorization 

To prove Theorems 1.2 and 1.4, we formulate an online variant of the matrix factorization optimization problem. We formulate both a row arrival model, suitable for applications to online query release, and a 

column arrival model, suitable for applications to online discrepancy minimization. Let us introduce the row arrival model first. At each time step a new row qt of an m × N matrix Q arrives. We treat qt as a row vector. An online matrix factorization algorithm receives this online sequence of rows, and maintains two growing matrices Lt and Rt such that Qt = LtRt, where Qt is the submatrix of Q consisting of the first t

8rows. Initially, we set R0 to be some arbitrary, possibly empty, matrix. At time step t, after receiving qt, the algorithm computes Rt, with the requirement that the rows of Rt−1 are a subset of the rows of Rt. Together with Rt, the algorithm also computes a row vector ℓt so that ℓtRt = qt. Note that the dimensions of ℓt

equals the number of rows of Rt, so the vectors ℓ1, . . . , ℓ t grow in dimension. The matrix Lt then consists of the rows ℓ1, . . . , ℓ t, suitably padded with 0 entries so that LtRt = Qt. We require that, for every t ∈ [m], 

∥Rt∥1→2 ≤ 1, i.e., that each column of Rt has ℓ2 norm at most 1. We say that the online factorization algorithm achieves factorization value γ for the matrix Q if max mt=1 ∥ℓt∥2 = ∥Lt∥2→∞ ≤ γ. Note that the value of m is not known to the algorithm. The column arrival online factorization model can be seen as “transpose” of the row arrival model. Here, at each time step a new column at of the matrix A (seen as column vector in Rm) arrives, and we maintain growing matrices Lt and Rt such that LtRt = At at all times t, where At is the matrix with columns a1, . . . , a t.The roles of Lt and Rt are swapped in the column arrival model. In particular, L0 is an arbitrary, potentially empty, initial left matrix, and at time step t we add columns to Lt−1 to form Lt, and compute a single column 

rt such that Ltrt = at; then Rt is the matrix with columns r1, . . . , r t, suitably padded with 0’s so that they are in the same dimension. Like the row arrival model, we still require that ∥Rt∥1→2 ≤ 1 at all times t, and we define the value of the factorization as γ = ∥LN ∥2→∞ , which is also an upper bound on ∥Lt∥2→∞ for all t.

## 2.2 Matrix Factorization Norms 

Factorization norms were introduced in functional analysis to study linear operators that factor through Hilbert space. Here, we focus on a special case that is most common in applications in computer science, and 12 give the definitions in terms of matrices. We define the γ2 factorization norm of an m × N matrix A as 

γ2(A) = min {∥ L∥2→∞ ∥R∥1→2 : LR = A}.

Recall that ∥R∥1→2 is the maximum ℓ2 norm of a column of R, and ∥L∥2→∞ is the maximum ℓ2 norm of a row of L. The γ2 norm satisfies many nice properties, e.g.: 

• γ2(A) = γ2(AT ); 

• γ2(B) ≤ γ2(A) for any submatrix B of A;

• γ2(A) is a norm on matrices; 

• γ2(A) can be approximated within any degree of accuracy in polynomial time given A as input. We refer to [MNT20] for proofs of these facts. Motivated by applications, mostly to differential privacy, related factorization norms have been defined. Here we also consider the γF norm [ENU20], defined as 

γF (A) = min {∥ L∥F ∥R∥1→2 : LR = A}.

Note that γF (A) ≤ √mγ 2(A) for any m × N matrix A. One can show that γF (A) is also a norm, and can be efficiently approximated in polynomial time, although we do not need these facts.

## 4.2 Reduction to Average Factorization 

In this section we build our reduction from online factorization to average online factorization. As mentioned in the introduction, the reduction is based on an idea of Muthukrishnan and Nikolov [ MN12 ]: to get a factorization certifying a bound on the γ2(Q) norm from an average factorization Q = LR where R is bounded only in Frobenius norm, we can remove from R the columns whose ℓ2 norm is larger than √2∥R∥F /√N , and factor only the corresponding columns of Q; the other columns of Q can then be factored recursively. Here, we implement the same approach in an online setting. To do so, we introduce two transformations that allow the algorithm to handle insertion and one-time deletion of input matrix columns. Then we use this ability to “kick out” columns whose current norm in the right matrix of the factorization is too large, and we handle these deleted columns recursively. We start with some necessary definitions. 

Definition 4.6. Given a matrix R with columns indexed by a set U , we define, for any x ∈ U , sR(x) to be the ℓ2 norm of the column indexed by x.

Our reduction will rely on the ability to insert and delete columns of a matrix whose rows arrive online. For us, deleting a column at a time step t will mean that we zero out the entries of this column in rows that arrive at step t or later. Inserting a column will have the conventional meaning of adding a column to the matrix. Next we define some useful notation that allows us to refer to these operations. 

Definition 4.7. For an m × N matrix Q with columns indexed by U , and a sequence U = ( U0, . . . , U m)

where U0, . . . , U m ⊆ U , define Q|U to be the matrix obtained by replacing all entries in the i-th row of Q

that do not correspond to elements of Ui by 0. Moreover, if U0 ⊆ U1 . . . ⊆ Um ⊆ U , we say this sequence is insertion-only. For a single set U ′ ⊆ U , let Q|U ′ be the matrix obtained by replacing all entries in columns not corresponding to elements of U ′ by 0.

Proof of Lemma 6.16. The proof proceeds in two steps.
