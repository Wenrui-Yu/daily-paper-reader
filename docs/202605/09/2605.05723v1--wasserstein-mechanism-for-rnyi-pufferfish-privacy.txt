Abstract 

This paper introduces the Œ±-Wasserstein mechanism for achieving R√©nyi Pufferfish Privacy using Laplace and Gaussian noise. By leveraging H√∂lder‚Äôs inequality, we demonstrate that the scale parameter of the Laplace mechanism can be calibrated via an upper bound on the WŒ± metric to satisfy (Œ±, œµ )-R√©nyi Pufferfish Privacy for Œ± ‚àà

(1 , ‚àû]. We show that at the limit Œ± = ‚àû, this framework recovers the established 

W‚àû mechanism for œµ-pufferfish privacy. This result is subsequently extended to the exponential mechanism. Furthermore, we propose a WŒ± mechanism for Gaussian noise for Œ± ‚àà (1 , ‚àû), demonstrating that it generalizes existing results within the R√©nyi Differential Privacy framework. Experimental evaluations reveal that our Œ±-Wasserstein mechanism significantly reduces noise power compared to the conventional W‚àû-based approach, with the Gaussian mechanism providing superior utility over the Laplace mechanism. Notably, the mechanisms derived in this work achieve exact (Œ±, œµ )-R√©nyi Pufferfish Privacy without requiring additional relaxations, such as Œ¥-approximations. 

1 Introduction 

Rooted in a rigorous mathematical framework of statistical indistinguishability, differential privacy provides a robust guarantee that the inclusion or exclusion of a single record remains probabilistically undetectable by bounding output variations within a privacy budget œµ [ 1‚Äì 3 ]. By ensuring that an adversary cannot reliably infer an individual‚Äôs presence or specific contribution from observable outputs, differential privacy has emerged as the gold standard for privacy-preserving data analysis. Due to its formal security properties, differential privacy is now widely deployed across various domains, including official statistics [4], machine learning [5] and healthcare [6]. The Pufferfish framework extends the principles of differential privacy to scenarios where the original data, such as a query response, exhibits probabilistic dependence on a secret [ 7, 8]. In this setting, the challenge lies in achieving statistical indistinguishability within the posterior data distribution following sanitization. To address this, the first noise calibration method was introduced by Song et al. (2017), who proposed setting the scale parameter b of zero-mean Laplace noise according to the ‚àû-order Wasserstein metric to satisfy œµ-pufferfish privacy [ 9]. However, computing the ‚àû-Wasserstein metric is complicated by its non-convex nature [ 10 , 11 ]. To resolve these computational difficulties, Ding (2022) introduced a 1-order Wasserstein (Kantorovich) approach for both Laplace and Gaussian noise mechanisms [12]. While strict privacy constraints often degrade data utility‚Äîa primary concern in differential privacy literature [ 13 , 14 ]‚Äîone may resort to relaxations such as R√©nyi measures. Similar to (œµ, Œ¥ )-differential privacy, these relaxations bound the probability of a data breach within specified limits. Building on the principles of R√©nyi Differential Privacy [ 15 ], œµ-pufferfish privacy has been extended to (Œ±, œµ )-R√©nyi Pufferfish Privacy (RPP) [ 16 ], which originally utilized a W‚àû mechanism scaled by the order 

Œ± [16 , Corollary 3.1]. However, because an order Œ± < ‚àû eases the stringent privacy requirements of 

œµ-pufferfish privacy, and given that Œ± plays a functionally identical role in the Wasserstein metric,

We review the Pufferfish privacy framework as originally proposed by Kifer and Machanavajjhala (2012, 2014) [ 7, 8], alongside its extension to the R√©nyi-divergence-based variant, R√©nyi Pufferfish Privacy (Pierquin et al., 2024 [ 16 ]). Assume that the data to be published, X (e.g., a query response or a column in a table), is statistically correlated with a sensitive secret S. Let PX|s,œÅ denote the conditional probability distribution of the data X given a secret instance S = s, where œÅ represents the adversary‚Äôs prior knowledge‚Äîsuch as the mean and covariance in the case of Gaussian-distributed data. In a multi-adversary environment, different agents may possess distinct prior beliefs œÅ. To preserve privacy, we transform X into a randomized output Y before publication. The adversary is assumed to have access only to this sanitized data Y , though they may attempt to infer individual secrets by analyzing aggregated statistics from repeated queries. Let S define a set of secret pairs (si, s j ) specified by the data curator. 3.1 Laplace Noise 

The Laplace mechanism was the inaugural method proposed for achieving differential privacy, introduced concurrently with the framework‚Äôs formal definition in [ 1 ]. Its prominence stems from the fact that the privacy requirement can be satisfied through straightforward arithmetic properties of the Laplace distribution. Consequently, it remains the most widely adopted additive noise mechanism across various extensions and variations of the differential privacy framework. In the context of pufferfish privacy, Song et al. [ 9] first demonstrated that calibrating the Laplace scale parameter to the 

‚àû-Wasserstein distance between discriminative secrets ensures œµ-pufferfish privacy‚Äîa result later extended to a Kantorovich ( W1) mechanism in [12]. Intuitively, this suggests that an Œ±-Wasserstein mechanism should exist for the R√©nyi Pufferfish Privacy framework. In this section, we derive a method for calibrating the scale parameter using the WŒ± metric to satisfy (Œ±, œµ )-R√©nyi pufferfish privacy, and we demonstrate that our approach generalizes the existing W‚àû mechanism.

4 Conclusion 

We investigated the calibration of Wasserstein mechanisms to achieve (Œ±, œµ )-R√©nyi pufferfish privacy. We proposed an Œ±-Wasserstein mechanism where the parameters for Laplace and Gaussian noise are calibrated using an upper-bounded WŒ± metric of the same order Œ±. Experimental results demonstrate that our Œ±-Wasserstein mechanism significantly reduces noise compared to existing W‚àû-based approaches. The results further verify that the Gaussian mechanism offers superior data utility over the Laplace mechanism when utilizing the R√©nyi divergence as a privacy relaxation. 

Discussion The primary results of this paper leverage H√∂lder‚Äôs inequality for the conjugate expo-nents Œ± and Œ±Œ±‚àí1 . This established technique is a staple of information theory, used in generalized error bounds [ 30 , 31 ] , entropy power inequalities [ 32 , 33 ], and foundational bounds on guessing entropy [ 34 , 35 ].. Furthermore, R√©nyi measures of order Œ±Œ±‚àí1 have recently gained prominence in information-theoretic privacy [ 36 , 37 ]. Beyond its core application to differential and pufferfish privacy, we highlight several promising extensions for future work.
