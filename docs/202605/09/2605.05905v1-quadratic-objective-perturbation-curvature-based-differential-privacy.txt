# Quadratic Objective Perturbation: Curvature-Based Differential Privacy 

## Daniel Cortild (daniel.cortild@maths.ox.ac.uk )Coralia Cartis (coralia.cartis@maths.ox.ac.uk )Mathematical Institute, University of Oxford, Oxford, United Kingdom 

Last Compiled: May 8, 2026 

Abstract 

Objective perturbation is a standard mechanism in differentially private empirical risk minimization. In par-ticular, Linear Objective Perturbation (LOP) enforces privacy by adding a random linear term, while strong convexity and stability are ensured by an additional deterministic quadratic term. However, this approach re-quires the strong assumption of bounded gradients of the loss function, which excludes many modern machine learning models. In this work, we introduce Quadratic Objective Perturbation (QOP), which perturbs the ob-jective with a random quadratic form. This perturbation induces strong convexity and enforces stability of the problem through curvature, thereby enabling privacy and allowing sensitivity to be controlled through spectral properties of the perturbation rather than assumptions on the gradients. As a result, we obtain ( ε, δ )-differential privacy under weaker assumptions, in the interpolation regime. Furthermore, we extend the analysis to account for approximate solutions, showing that privacy guarantees are preserved under inexact solves. Additionally, we derive utility guarantees in terms of empirical excess risk, and provide a theoretical and numerical comparison to LOP, highlighting the advantages of curvature-based perturbations. Finally, we discuss algorithmic aspects and show that the resulting problems can be solved efficiently using modern splitting schemes. 

Keywords. Differential Privacy, Objective Perturbation, Random Matrix Theory, Three-Operator Splitting.

In many applications, the parameter space is naturally constrained, for instance to enforce structural properties such as boundedness or feasibility. From a theoretical perspective, the constraint helps ensure well-posedness in the absence of strong convexity. This formulation captures a wide range of problems, from classical linear models to modern overparametrized systems. In many such settings, especially in large dimensions, the problem exhibits many difficulties; minimizers are in general not unique, curvature may be highly anisotropic, and classical regularity assumptions such as strong convexity or bounded gradients often fail. We refer to Negahban et al. (2012) and the references therein for a more complete overview. Empirical risk minimization is often performed on sensitive data, which we aim to protect through the framework of 

Differential Privacy (DP) (Dwork, 2006). Informally, DP guarantees that an adversary cannot reliably infer whether any particular individual contributed to the dataset. Achieving this guarantee, however, comes at a price in terms of utility, especially in the absence of strong regularity, in which case the minimizers might be highly sensitive to perturbations in the data. Balancing privacy and utility has been a central challenge since the introduction of DP, and our work places itself within this line of research. Existing approaches mostly rely on the strong assumption of bounded gradients of the loss functions, which excludes many models and is unrealistic in modern machine learning. A core contribution is that we do not make such an assumption. 1

Furthermore, this approach requires the strong regularity assumption of bounded gradients of the loss functions to achieve privacy, to ensure limited individual contributions of each datapoint. The strong assumptions required in the above works suggest that linear perturbations are fundamentally insufficient in our setting, motivating the need for an alternative perturbation mechanism.

The key feature is that the same random perturbation simultaneously provides privacy, controls the stability of the problem, and induces strong convexity. Unlike the previously mentioned linear perturbations, which shift the minimizers, the random quadratic perturbation modifies the curvature of the objective, ensuring the sensitivity of the output is controlled through the curvature of the objective rather than through assumptions on the gradients. As a result, we can guarantee privacy without requiring bounded gradients of the loss functions. Besides objective perturbation, other ideas have been developed to ensure private ERM. We highlight output perturbation, which adds noise to the final solution (Dwork, 2006; Chaudhuri et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2017; Lowy and Razaviyayn, 2021), gradient perturbation, which injects noise throughout the algorithm (Bassily et al., 2014; Song et al., 2013; Abadi et al., 2016; Bassily et al., 2019), functional perturbation, which perturbs a representation of the objective (Zhang et al., 2012), input perturbation, which perturbs the data prior to training (Duchi et al., 2013; Fukuchi et al., 2017), and sampling-based methods, such as the exponential mechanism, which selects outputs at random proportionally to the exponential utility score (McSherry and Talwar, 2007). 

Contributions. Our contributions may be summarized as follows: 1. We introduce a new objective perturbation mechanism based on random quadratic perturbations, eliminating the need for bounded gradient assumptions required by prior work. 2. We develop a novel privacy analysis showing that the quadratic perturbation controls the sensitivity of the out-put through curvature, yielding ( ε, δ )-privacy under weaker assumptions than prior work, in the interpolation regime. 3. We extend the method to approximate solves while preserving meaningful privacy guarantees. 4. We provide utility guarantees by deriving bounds on the empirical excess risk.

## 2.2 Differential Privacy 

We use the notion of ( ε, δ )-differential privacy, introduced in Dwork (2006). Intuitively, a randomized mechanism provides DP if it produces similar outputs on similar datasets. The following definitions formalize the notion of 

similar datasets and of differential privacy .

Definition 2.2 (Replacement Adjacency) .

We say two datasets D and D′ are replacement adjacent , denoted by D ∼ D ′, if they have the same number of datapoints and differ in at most one of them. 

Definition 2.3 (Differential Privacy) .

We say a mechanism M provides ( ϵ, δ )-differential privacy if, for any two datasets D ∼ D ′ and any measurable set S ⊂ ran( M), it holds that 

P(M(D) ∈ S) ≤ eε · P(M(D′) ∈ S) + δ, 

where M(D) and M(D′) are the outputs of M under the data D and D′.Much work on differential privacy has been carried out since its introduction, and many results about enforcing privacy have since been introduced. We present some useful results that we will make use of in our main proof in Appendix B. For an overview of these results and a more thorough introduction to differential privacy we refer to Dwork and Roth (2014) and the references therein.

Proof Idea. We provide a proof idea here, and the full details are available in Appendix E. We note that the perturbed objective function does not necessarily have a unique minimizer. We thus condition the mechanism on W having uniformly bounded below eigenvalues, making the perturbed objective strongly convex and hence have a unique minimizer. As this occurs with high probability, the Conditional Mechanism Lemma B.3 guarantees that privacy is preserved. In order to prove that the release of θfinal is private, we shall show that the joint release of θpriv and θfinal − θpriv is private, by applying the Composition of Mechanisms Lemma B.2. The latter two releases imply the first release, and hence can only worsen privacy. This idea is inspired by Iyengar et al. (2019). As such, the proof is split into two parts, proving the privacy of the release of θpriv and proving the privacy of the release of θfinal − θpriv ;1. To show that the release of θpriv is private, we first show it is private under a simplification assumption, namely that the regularizer r is twice differentiable and that the constraint set C = Rd. This is the main novelty of the proof, presented in Lemma E.2, and largely extends the ideas in Chaudhuri et al. (2011) and Kifer et al. (2012). We then apply the Successive Approximation Lemma B.4 to remove the simplification assumptions one-by-one, as done in Kifer et al. (2012). This is described in Lemma E.3. 2. To show that the release of θfinal − θpriv is private, it is sufficient to note that θfinal − θpriv = θapp − θpriv + b,where b is Gaussian noise. In this step, we additionally condition the mechanism on W having eigenvalues 5uniformly bounded by above. In this case, by the Gaussian Mechanism B.1, the process is private if θapp −θpriv 

is bounded uniformly in the noise realization, which it is. We then uncondition on the additional conditional event, introducing a slightly larger failure probability. This is described in Lemma E.4. Theorem 3.1 shows that privacy is ensured by combining two mechanisms: the random quadratic perturbation, which controls the sensitivity of the exact minimizer through curvature, and the Gaussian mechanism, which accounts for approximation errors.

# 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we introduced Quadratic Objective Perturbation (QOP) for differentially private empirical risk min-imization. In contrast with Linear Objective Perturbation (LOP), which relies on strong bounded gradient as-sumptions, our mechanism enforces privacy through curvature while simultaneously inducing stability and strong convexity, yielding ( ε, δ )-differential privacy guarantees under significantly weaker assumptions. 9We established privacy and utility guarantees, extended the analysis to approximate solutions, and showed that the resulting problem can be solved efficiently due to its composite structure. Our theoretical and numerical results demonstrate that QOP and LOP satisfy fundamentally different scaling laws when the gradients are unbounded. In particular, LOP degrades with the constraint diameter, whereas QOP remains stable. These findings suggest that curvature-based perturbations provide a natural alternative to classical objective perturbation methods, particularly in modern machine learning settings where strong regularity assumptions are rarely met or unrealistic. 

Future Work. Several directions remain open. A first avenue would be to relax the assumption of a common minimizer of the loss functions, thus extending the applicability of our framework. Another direction is to strengthen the privacy analysis by considering R´ enyi differential privacy rather than standard differential privacy.

## G.2 Stochastic Three-Operator Splitting 

While the proposed mechanism is conceptually simple, it requires solving a perturbed problem which is nontrivial due to its finite-sum structure, nonsmooth regularizer, and constraints. In this section, we show that the perturbed problem can be solved efficiently by leveraging its composite structure. We consider a realization of W , and focus on how to (approximately) solve Problem (P-Priv-App). Specifically, the problem will be fixed, and the randomness will arise through the algorithm alone. 24 A key observation is that Problem (P-Priv-App) may be written as the sum of a smooth finite-sum problem with two convex regularizers, namely argmin θ∈Rd

(θ − ˜θ∗)T W (θ − ˜θ∗) is smooth and convex, and ιC is the indicator of the convex set C.We note this is not specific to our resulting problems, and would also be the structure of Problem (P-Priv-Lin), with ˜ℓ(θ; z) = ℓ(θ; z) + aT θn + ∆2n ∥θ∥2.This structure suggests the use of the Three-Operator Splitting scheme (Davis and Yin, 2017). The scheme was originally introduced in the deterministic setting, treating the finite-sum as a single operator, and has since been extended in the stochastic setting, in which a random term of the sum is used at each iteration (Yurtsever et al., 2016, 2021; Cortild and Cartis, 2026). The resulting algorithm is described in Algorithm StoTOS. 

Algorithm StoTOS (Stochastic Three-Operator Splitting) 

Require: a step-size ρ, a relaxation sequence ( λk), and an initial guess x0 ∈ Rd.

for k = 0 , . . . , K do 

Draw ik ∈ { 1, . . . , n } randomly and independently. Set θk = prox ρr (xk). Set zk = proj C (2 θk − xk − ρn ∇˜ℓ(θk; zik )). Set xk+1 = xk − λkθk + λkzk.Update k → k + 1. 

end for return θK

The work Yurtsever et al. (2016) introduced stochasticity into the scheme for strongly convex objectives, whereas Yurtsever et al. (2021) studied non-strongly convex objectives. Both works make a strong assumption resembling uniformly bounded variance of the stochastic oracle, which is not verified in our setting without additional assump-tions. In contrast, Cortild and Cartis (2026) rely on a weaker assumption, by only assuming a finite variance of the stochastic oracle at a solution of the problem. Convergence to a solution of the perturbed problem can be established in several important cases. In particular, when the constraint set C is an affine subspace (including the unconstrained setting), the method provably converges with accompanying rate statements. More broadly, more general conditions are considered in Cortild and Cartis (2026). We also note that when the problem is unconstrained, the function ιC is identically zero, and can therefore be disregarded. In this case, the Three-Operator Splitting algorithm reduces to the Backward-Forward algorithm (Attouch et al., 2018), and Algorithm StoTOS would reduce to a stochastic variant of it, in which the projection step is trivial. 

## G.3 Experimental Setup 

The goal of the experiment in Section 5.2 is to isolate the dependence of the mechanisms on the constraint diameter 

κ, and thereby the dependence on the bounded gradients assumption required by LOP. 

Data Generation. To generate the data, we generate n independent samples xi ∼ N (0 , I ), and rescale them to lie within [ −ξ, ξ ]d. We then generate a random θ∗ ∼ N (0 , I ), and define yi = xTi θ∗, thus forcing the interpolation condition to hold. We then make use of ˜θ∗ = θ∗ + ˜ c, where ˜ c ∼ N (0 , 0.1I) is a randomly drawn vector. We consider (n, d ) = (300 , 100) and ξ = 5. 

Problem Setup. We consider the regularization parameter ω = 1, and constraint sets C = [ −κ, κ ]d for varying 

κ > 0. 

Algorithmic Setup. We solve the resulting problem through Algorithm StoTOS. For this purpose, we run 1000 iterations using the relaxation parameters λk = ( k + 1) −1/2−2·0.001 and the step-size ρ = 1 / Lip( n∇˜ℓ). 25 Mechanism Setup. We consider the privacy budget ( ε, δ ) = (0 .5, 0.01). When running either mechanism, we omit the final Gaussian noise term b, as it affects both mechanisms identically and does not impact the comparison. We perform the following split: ( ε1, ε 2) = ( ε, 0) and ( δ1, δ 2, δ 3, δ 4) = ( δ/ 2, 0, δ/ 2, 0). We select the Wishart hidden dimension to be m = 2 d. For LOP-Clip, we clip each gradient to have norm at most 10000. 

Evaluation Protocol. For both cases, namely with and without interpolation, we evaluate the empirical risk. We run the algorithm 10 times and report the average. 

Computer Resources. The experiment is written in Python 3.13, and was executed on an Apple Silicon MacBook Pro, with a M5 chip and 16GB of RAM.
