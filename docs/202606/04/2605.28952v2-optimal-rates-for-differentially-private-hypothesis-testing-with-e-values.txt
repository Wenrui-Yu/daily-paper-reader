## Abstract 

E-values have attracted considerable interest in recent years as flexible tools for enabling anytime-valid and adaptive data analysis. Hypothesis testing is at the core of many of these applications, which can often involve private or sensitive data. In this work, we answer a simple but important question: given two distributions P

and Q, what is the maximum achievable e-power when testing X ‚àº Pn against 

X ‚àº Qn with e-values that satisfy Œµ-differential privacy? We characterize the optimal rate for this problem and provide an algorithm which matches it exactly. In the sequential setting, when observations arrive one-by-one and the analyst chooses when to halt, we give matching upper and lower bounds on the stopping times of any private e-process. Numerical experiments confirm the practicality of our algorithms, which require less data than the recently proposed DP-SPRT across a range of sequential testing problems and privacy levels. 

## 1 Introduction 

Hypothesis testing is ubiquitous in modern scientific practice and also one of the most fundamental problems in statistical inference. We consider simple hypothesis testing: an algorithm receives a dataset X ‚àà X n and must evaluate whether it was sampled from a distribution P (the null hypothesis) or Q (the alternative). In the classical, decision-theoretic setting, we wish to output a single decision 

œï(X) ‚àà { 0, 1}, where the output œï = 1 is interpreted as rejecting the null hypothesis in favor of the alternate. To prevent false discoveries, we ask that P[œï(X) = 1] ‚â§ Œ± for some fixed significance level Œ±. The performance of the test is then measured by its true-positive rate or power , Q[œï(X) = 1] .Recently, a complementary paradigm has emerged based on the concept of e-values [31 ]. Instead of binary decisions, algorithms for hypothesis testing in this setting output a real-valued, non-negative value E which quantifies the degree to which X provides evidence for the alternate hypothesis over the null. We call E an e-variable and refer to realizations of E as e-values , but gloss over this distinction when speaking informally. Larger e-values present more evidence for the alternative, because we expect the e-value to be small under the null, as per its definition. 

Definition 1.1. An e-variable for P is a non-negative random variable E satisfying EP[E] ‚â§ 1.

A major advantage of this approach is that statistical inferences drawn from (products of) e-values often remain valid even when decisions about data collection and experimental design are made adaptively, whereas those same conditions typically invalidate type-I error control in classical methods [ 31 , 17 ]. There is thus good reason to hope that more widespread use of e-values and their sequential analog, e-processes, could mitigate issues related to the misuse of p-values which have contributed to the ongoing scientific reproducibility crisis [ 40 , 2 , 36 , 17 , 33 , 8, 18 ]. Methods based on e-values are also broadly useful for modern statistical workflows in which the number and timing of analyses are rarely fixed in advance such as adaptive clinical trials [ 38 ], online active experimentation [48, 25], online multiple testing [49], sequential change detection [37] and auditing [46, 7, 16]. Many statistical applications are driven by sensitive individual-level data, however. This motivates the need for methods which additionally satisfy differential privacy (DP), a rigorous and widely used definition of data privacy which guarantees that the result of a statistical analysis cannot depend too much on any one person‚Äôs data [14, 15, 1]. 

Definition 1.3. For Œµ ‚â• 0, an algorithm M : X n ‚Üí O is Œµ-differentially private if for all datasets 

X and X‚Ä≤ differing in one entry and all S ‚äÜ O we have Pr[ M(X) ‚àà S] ‚â§ eŒµ Pr[ M(X‚Ä≤) ‚àà S].

There is a large body of literature investigating differentially private hypothesis testing in the classical decision-theoretic setting [ 6 , 42 , 24 , 34 , 45 , 39 , 9 ], including several works that study sequential testing problems [ 11 , 50 , 27 , 29 , 4]. We are aware of only three papers on differential privacy and e-values. Waudby-Smith et al. [47] and Saha and Ramdas [35] consider local DP, wherein privacy protections are applied to individual data points before aggregation [ 23 ]. But while the privacy guarantees provided by local DP are very strong, they can also come at the cost of substantial utility degradation and are not appropriate for all statistical applications. Our work therefore focuses on the more commonly used central model of DP, where the privacy protections apply to the output of a trusted aggregator. To our knowledge, the only work that considers e-values in this setting is that of Csillag and Mesquita [10] . The algorithms described in that work only apply to testing distributions P and Q with bounded likelihood ratios, however, which fails to hold even in the simple case of testing Gaussians. We aim to provide a more complete understanding of DP e-values for hypothesis testing. Within this context, our major contributions are: ‚Ä¢ An instance-specific hardness result describing the maximum possible e-power achievable by any Œµ-DP e-value when testing P against Q (Theorem 2.2). ‚Ä¢ The construction of an Œµ-DP e-value which achieves optimal e-power for any arbitrary pair of distributions, up to lower order terms (Theorem 2.4). ‚Ä¢ An instance-specific lower-bound on the expected stopping time of any sequential test based on thresholding an Œµ-DP e-process, i.e., the amount of data which must be collected on average to achieve a given level of statistical power (Theorem 3.1). ‚Ä¢ An efficient algorithm for computing Œµ-DP e-processes which simultaneously matches this bound, up to a constant multiplicative factor, for all stopping times larger than some tunable minimal threshold (Algorithm 1, Theorem 3.2). In Section 4, we compare Algorithm 1 against the recently proposed DP-SPRT of Michel et al. [27] and find that our algorithm achieves consistently earlier stopping times across a range of settings. Given the central importance of simple hypothesis tests, our results also provide a foundation for investigating many other interesting problems involving robust, adaptive, and privacy-preserving statistical methods based on e-values. For instance, a natural next step would be to investigate Œµ-DP e-values for composite hypothesis testing; in that setting, it is often possible to directly apply hardness results for simple hypothesis testing, such as our Theorem 2.2, via so-called least-favorable pairs [ 21 ]. A similar idea arises in statistical estimation, where the difficulty of estimating a parameter Œ∏ to error ¬±Œ± is strongly connected to worst-case difficulty of testing a distribution DŒ∏ against DŒ∏‚Ä≤ for 

|Œ∏ ‚àíŒ∏‚Ä≤| ‚â§ Œ± [12 ]. It is therefore likely that the ideas we present could be extended to better understand 2the complexity of e.g. anytime-valid confidence sequences [ 32 , 33 ] under central DP, much as the foundational work of Canonne et al. [6] on private simple hypothesis testing in the decision-theoretic setting helped contribute to breakthroughs in instance-optimal DP estimation algorithms [26, 3].

The formal definition of differentially private e-variables requires some care: DP is a property of a randomized algorithm M while the e-variable property is about the output E = M(X), a random variable. We draw a clear distinction between these two objects.

If f is randomized, then the expectation is taken with respect to both the input data and the internal randomness of f unless otherwise stated. We mainly state our results in terms of continuous distributions and ignore measure-theoretic details in the main body of the paper. We collect a number of standard definitions and lemmas related to differential privacy, information theory, and e-values in Appendix A. Formal proofs of most of our results are deferred to Appendix D.

In sequential hypothesis testing, new data is collected gradually over time until some condition is met and the experiment is concluded. The number of data points used to make any final decision is therefore a random variable, N , called a stopping time . It is typical for E[N ] to be much smaller than the minimum n required in the batch setting [ 44 ]; in fact, the Sequential Probability Ratio Test (SPRT) [ 43 ] was first developed during World War II in response to the US Navy‚Äôs need for efficient alternatives to the likelihood ratio test. Much like the likelihood ratio test, however, the SPRT is decision-theoretic and requires the analyst to commit to a stopping condition in advance. The analogue in our setting is the e-process , which is a sequence of e-variables E1, E 2, . . . with the additional property that EN is itself an e-variable for any stopping time N . This leads to some very useful properties, such as optional stopping (we may stop the experiment at any time and draw valid conclusions from our final output) as well as optional continuation (if our results are inconclusive, a second scientist can run a follow-up experiment and merge the resulting e-values by multiplication). The theory of e-processes is intimately connected with that of (non-negative) martingales, which have an interesting game-theoretic interpretation as the accumulated wealth of a gambler betting against the null [ 36 ]. We collect several standard definitions and results about martingales in Appendix A. Mitzenmacher and Upfal [28 , Chapter 12 ] provide an accessible introduction to the tools we require.

## 4 Simulations 

In this section, we compare our Algorithm 1 against a private variant of the sequential proba-bility ratio test (SPRT) [ 43 ]. The non-private SPRT accumulates the log-likelihood ratio Lt =Pti=1 log( q(Xi)/p (Xi)) and stops when Lt crosses upper or lower boundaries, which are selected to obtain type I and II errors of Œ±, Œ≤ . Existing DP sequential testing methods implement variants of the AboveThreshold algorithm [ 14 ] to privately check if Lt has crossed the boundaries. We compare against the recently proposed DP-SPRT of Michel et al. [27] , which is restricted to testing single-parameter exponential families with bounded support. (The other natural baseline of Zhang et al. [50] only satisfies approximate DP.) Our evaluation uses the distributions P = Bernoulli(0 .3) and Q = Bernoulli( q), which satisfy the assumptions of DP-SPRT and were also used in the evaluation of Michel et al. [27] . Our e-process construction is one-sided, controlling rejection of a designated null, whereas DP-SPRT is a two-sided test. To obtain a two-sided decision rule, we run two private e-processes in parallel: one for testing 

P against Q and one for Q against P. The e-processes use competitive ratio œÅ = 3 and a privacy parameter of Œµ/ 2 each. For each method, we use levels Œ± = Œ≤ = 140 for type I and II error control and report the empirical CDFs of the stopping time N after 100 trials. Across the settings shown in Figure 2, we consistently find that our two-sided private e-process stops earlier than DP-SPRT. This is despite the fact that our algorithm makes no parametric assumptions about the distributions being tested, whereas the DP-SPRT was specifically designed for Bernoulli data. While it is impossible to draw strong conclusions on the basis of a single experiment, these results are nevertheless a promising sign for the practical usefulness of our algorithms, as well as for DP e-values and e-processes more generally.
