Abstract 

Emergent intelligence have played a major role in the modern AI development, manifesting as previously unobserved capabilities enabled by scaling model parame-ters. While existing studies primarily rely on empirical observations to characterize this phenomenon, a rigorous theoretical framework remains underexplored. This study attempts to develop a mathematical approach to formalize emergent intelli-gence from the perspective of limit theory. Specifically, we introduce a performance function E(N, P, K ), dependent on data size N , model size P and training steps 

K, to quantify intelligence behavior. We posit that intelligence emerges as a transition from finite to effectively infinite knowledge, and thus recast emergent intelligence as the existence of the limit lim N,P,K →∞ E(N, P, K ), with emer-gent abilities corresponding to the limiting behavior. Moreover, we show that the scaling laws of foundation models are approximately governed by the convergence rate of E(N, P, K ) to its asymptotic value E(∞, ∞, ∞). This limit theory helps reveal that emergent intelligence originates from the existence of a parameter-limit architecture (referred to as the limit architecture), and that emergent intelligence rationally corresponds to the learning behavior of this limit system. By introducing tools from nonlinear Lipschitz operator theory, including charac-teristic constant Lip (T ) of Lipschitz operator, theory of Lipschitz dual operator and spectral theory of compact operators, we prove that the necessary and suf-ficient conditions for existence of the limit architecture is (1) Lip (Ti) ≤ 1 for all i > K , for some constant K; (2) there exists a projection operator T such that ∥Ti − T ∥ = ϵi, where P∞

## 1 Introduction 

Foundation models [ 4] have revolutionized the AI community across a wide range of domains, including natural language processing [ 5– 9], computer vision [ 10 – 13 ], multimodal learning [ 8, 14 – 17 ], graph learning [ 18 , 19 ], and programming languages [ 20 ]. These models demonstrate remarkable capabilities in understanding human instructions, exhibiting knowledge and logical reasoning, and even aligning with human values, thereby paving a promising path toward general artificial intelligence [ 21 ]. The crux behind these successes lies the phenomenon of ermergent intelligence in foundation models [ 22 –24 ]. The concept of emergence has its origins in physics and biology [ 25 – 27 ]. A central insight is captured by the principle of “More Is Different” articulated by Nobel laureate Philip Anderson [ 25 ]. This idea suggests that “quantitative changes in a system can lead to qualitative changes in behavior” adapted from [28]. Recently, Wei et al. [ 22 ] provided a clear definition of emergent ability in large language models (LLMs) as: “abilities that are not present in smaller-scale models but are present in large-scale models; thus they cannot be predicted by simply extrapolating the performance improvements on smaller-scale models”. Such emergent abilities were first discovered in the GPT-3 family [ 5], and further motivate the costly effort to train ever larger models on ever larger datasets, in the hope of unlocking new capabilities. It is now widely recognized that scaling up foundation models (e.g., data size, model size, and training steps) can consistently lead to improved performance and sample efficiency across a wide range of downstream tasks. Empirically, the generalization loss of foundation models has been observed to follow a predictable power-law relationship with respect to data size, model size and training steps, a property commonly referred to as the neural scaling law [ 29 ]. In practice, to reduce the cost of discovering effective training strategies, researchers typically validate ideas through small-scale experiments and then extrapolate their performance to larger regimes using scaling laws. By leveraging such reliable extrapolation, it becomes possible to iterate efficiently at smaller scales while identifying the approaches most likely to perform optimally in full-scale training. As a result, neural scaling laws have become a standard principle in the development of state-of-the-art foundation models, including Chinchilla 70B [ 30 ], PaLM 540B [9], GPT-4 [6], and many others [31–39]. Although emergent abilities and neural scaling laws have been empirically vali-dated across a wide range of foundation models, theoretical understanding of these 2phenomena remains limited. In this paper, we attempt to propose a rigorous math-ematical framework to analyze ermergent intelligence and scaling laws. Our central observation is that intelligence arises as an emergent phenomenon characterized by a transition from finite knowledge to effectively infinite knowledge. This perspective naturally motivates the possibility of using limit theory to formalize ermergent intelli-gence, as fingding irrational numbers through limit of rational numbers. Specifically, we introduce a three-dimensional function E(N, P, K ) to quantify the intelligent behav-ior (i.e., generalization capability) of foundation models, where N represents the data size (i.e., the number of data points for training foundation models), P represents the model size (i.e., the number of model parameters), and K represents the training steps of optimization algorithms (closely related to the amount of computation). To understand ermergent intelligence, it is essential to study the asymptotic behavior of E(N, P, K ) in the limit as N → ∞ , P → ∞ , and K → ∞ , corresponding to the regime of infinite knowledge, architecture and training steps. Under this formulation, ermergent intelligence can then be rationally characterized by the existence of the limit lim N,P,K →∞ E(N, P, K ). Furthermore, when this limit exists (denoted by E(∞, ∞, ∞)), the scaling laws of foundation models can be explicitly revealed by the rate at which 

E(N, P, K ) converges to E(∞, ∞, ∞). To characterize foundation models in the infinite-parameter regime, we introduce an infinite-dimensional system termed the limit architecture , defined as the infinite composition of stacked basic functional blocks. The properties and behavior of this limit architecture, as quantified by its generalization performance ε(∞, ∞, ∞), pro-vide fundamental insights into the mechanisms underlying ermergent intelligence in foundation models. We introduce the Lip constant, Lip (T ), as a key metric to characterize the existence of the limit architecture, and establish necessary and suf-ficient conditions under which such a limit exists. Moreover, we propose a standard error decomposition of ε(N, P, K ) − ε(∞, ∞, ∞), which separates the total error into three components: the optimization (weight) error ε(N, P, K ) − ε(N, P, ∞),

the architecture error ε(N, P, ∞) − ε(N, ∞, ∞), and the statistical (sample) error 

ε(N, ∞, ∞) − ε(∞, ∞, ∞). We further formalize the conditions under which these error components arise and establish corresponding upper bounds, which enable us to derive explicit scaling laws with respect to data size, model size, and training steps. In summary, our theoretical findings are mainly three-fold: 

• Ermergent intelligence. The existence of ermergent intelligence critically depends on the properties of the basic building blocks. Specifically, let {Ti}, i = 1 , 2, · · · ,

denote the sequence of basic blocks in a foundation model. These blocks satisfy the self-mapping condition, and there exists a constant K such that Lip (Ti) ≤ 1 for all i > K . Moreover, the sequence {Ti} converges to a projection operator T , i.e., 

Ti → T , with ∥Ti − T ∥ ∈ ℓ1, which corresponds to a condensing property . We show that these conditions are necessary and sufficient for the existence of ermergent intelligence, and under the conditions, the limit architecture of foundation models can be well approximated by a finite composition of stacked basic functional blocks. 

• Scaling laws. We employ the standard error decomposition to analyze the discrep-ancy between E(N, P, K ) and E(∞, ∞, ∞), and provide the upper bounds estimation for the weight error, architecture error and sample error separately. The bounds 3illustrate that the scaling laws of training steps and model size follow exponential law, and scaling law of data size follows a power law. 

• Empirical validation. Experimental results demonstrate that the Lip constant effectively characterizes the properties of basic blocks and serves as a powerful criterion for evaluating the usability of network architectures, i.e., yielding a stable architecture. Empirical analysis on state-of-the-art open-source foundation models further supports the condensing property as a core condition for the existence of ermergent intelligence. The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Related work is reviewed in Section 2. The formulation of ermergent intelligence and scaling laws from a limit-theoretic perspective is presented in Section 3. Section 4 introduces the main theoretical tools, including the standard error decomposition framework, the limit architecture, and the Lip constant. The necessary and sufficient conditions for the existence of the limit architecture are established in Section 5. Sections 6 and 7 present the existence conditions for ermergent intelligence and the corresponding scaling laws, respectively. Experimental validations of the theoretical results are provided in Sections 8 and 9. Finally, we conclude with a discussion of open problems. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Emergence Ability in Foundation Models. 

Emergence as an idea has long been discussed in domains such as system science, physics and biology [ 25 – 27 ], etc. For example, Anderson [ 25 ] states that “emergent abilities for complex systems are those properties that (1) emerge at each level of complexity and (2) cannot be understood simply by analyzing the single components ‘behavior’ ”. Hopfield [ 40 ] studies emergent properties in simple-structured neural networks with neurons having elementary properties, and states that “computational properties of use to biological organisms or the construction of computers can emerge as collective properties of systems having a large number of simple equivalent components (or neurons)”. For foundation models, Wei et al., [ 22 ] adds two important concepts based on previous definitions, including the unpredictability and the magnitude of the performance increase, and states that “An ability is emergent if it is not present in smaller models but is present in larger models. Emergent abilities would not have been directly predicted by extrapolating a scaling law from small-scale models. When visualized via a scaling curve, emergent abilities show a clear pattern–performance is near-random until a certain critical threshold of scale is reached, after which performance increases to substantially above random”. This definition should be the first LLM-specific definition and has been mostly most used in the current academic literatures [24 , 41 , 42 ]. Recent work [ 41 ] shows that the seemingly sharp and unpredictable emergence of capabilities is largely driven by the use of nonlinear evaluation metrics, such as accuracy. In contrast, when performance is assessed using linear metrics (e.g., token edit distance), model improvements tend to be smooth and predictable. Two different approaches have been adopted to analyze ermergent intelligence. In the complex system science [ 43 ], specific dynamic models are used to characterize 4the behavior of emergence. Scaling is often applied to emergence in complex systems to demonstrate how “more is different”, and emergent abilities could be understood through an analogy to phase transitions, e.g., [ 44 – 47 ]. These methods often analyze emergent property with the temporal variable, while foundation models primarily focus on variables like model size P , data size D and training steps T , often rendering them unsuitable for direct application in this context. Besides, the “broken symmetry” assumption in physics [ 25 ], grounded in a specific “material basis”, does not lend itself directly to modeling the ermergent intelligence of foundation models. For foundation models, some studies explore to use different tools to help under-stand emergent abilities, e.g., analyzing loss function and training dynamics [ 39 ], quantization[ 48 ], task complexity [ 49 ], etc. Besides, emergent abilities are manifested through specific capabilities, such as in-context learning [ 50 – 52 ], symbolic abstrac-tion [ 53 ], RL-enhanced reasoning [ 54 – 56 ], LLMs-powered agents [ 57 , 58 ], etc. Existing approaches to ermergent intelligence have been predominantly empirical, with rela-tively little work on rigorous mathematical foundations. In this paper, we make an attempt to develop a preliminary mathematical framework for formalizing ermergent intelligence from the perspective of limit theory. 

2.2 Scaling Law in Foundation Models 

Scaling laws have been proposed to analyze the behavior of deep learning across diverse domains and tasks. Investigations into the relationship between generalization error, training data size, and model capacity date back to before the era of deep learning [59 ]. For deep neural networks, Hestness et al. [ 60 ] observes that performance improves according to a power-law scaling behavior across a variety of domains, including machine translation, language modeling, image processing, and speech recognition. Importantly, these scaling relationships are shown to persist across model improvements. Recently, Kaplan et al. [ 29 ] pushes the scale of these studies further into large language models, studying power laws for models up to 1.5B parameters trained on 23B tokens to determine the optimal allocation of a fixed compute budget. Subsequent studies [ 30 ] revisit this issue and find that Kaplan scaling law significantly underestimate the amount of data required for optimal training, although substantial procedural differences make it difficult to pinpoint the source of this discrepancy. As a result, many subsequent LLMs [ 7, 33 ] have been trained following the Chinchilla scaling laws [ 30 ]. Besiroglu et al. [ 34 ] revisit the methodology of Hoffmann et al. [ 30 ] and report that the confidence intervals in the original study appear implausibly narrow. In a related line of research, Porian et al. [ 61 ] examine discrepancies between the scaling laws proposed by Kaplan et al. [29] and those derived under the Chinchilla framework [30]. Since then, researchers have investigated various aspects of scaling in language models. Hernandez et al. [ 62 ] analyze scaling laws governing transfer across distributions in fine-tuning settings. Aghajanyan et al. [ 63 ] explore scaling behaviors in multimodal foundation models. Poli et al. [ 64 ] extend scaling studies to hybrid architectures such as Mamba [ 65 ], demonstrating the effectiveness of this emerging model family. Krajewski et al. [ 66 ] characterize differences in scaling properties between dense transformers and mixture of expert models. Gadre et al. [ 67 ] show that the loss of over-trained models, trained past compute-optimality, is predictable, and propose a scaling law relating loss 5to average downstream task performance. [ 68 ] revisits scaling laws by examining the impact of data quality and training strategies on model performance. A key limitation of existing scaling laws is their disregard for inference costs, which dominate the long-term expenses of utilizing large models in real-world applications. Sardana et al. [ 69 ]modify the Chinchilla scaling laws to account for both the computational and real-world costs of inference. The growing adoption of LLMs in reasoning systems also highlights the need for scaling frameworks that explicitly account for inference costs [ 55 , 70 –72 ]. Recently, some theoretical studies of scaling laws are proposed. The scaling law for linear models with structured (nonisotropic) covariates, including infinite-width kernel regression, have been analyzed using tools from statistical physics and random matrix theory [ 73 – 78 ]. For example, Mei et al. [ 78 ] analyze a linear model with random projections of isotropic covariates. Bahri et al. [ 76 ] consider a linear teacher-student model under a power-law spectrum assumption on the covariates, and they showed that the test loss of the ordinary least square estimator decreases following a power law in data size when the model size is infinite. Furthermore, some researches analyze (precise) generalization error of simple closed-form estimators such as ridge regression [79–82]. While they study the limiting effects of model size and data size, some works also study the dependence on training steps. Bordelon et al. [ 83 ] consider a linear random feature model and analyze the test loss of the solution found by (batch) gradient flow. They focused on the bottleneck regimes where two of the data size, model size and traning steps are infinite, and showed that the risk has a power-law decay in the remaining quantity. Lin et al. [ 84 ] consider a linear predictor trained by one-pass SGD with geometrically decaying stepsizes, and demonstrate that small variance error is due to the implicit regularization effect of SGD. High dimensional limits of SGD have been analyzed with Volterra integral equations in the offline case [ 85 ] or with recursive matrix equations in the online case [ 86 ]. Based on this theoretical foundation, [ 87 ]uses phase-plane analysis to characterize scaling laws in the compute-limited, infinite-data regime. Li et al. [ 88 ] establish a functional scaling law that captures the full loss trajectory under arbitrary learning rate schedules. Besides, existing scaling analyses for the additive setting [ 89 , 90 ] explicitly decompose the loss into an independent sum, simplifying the analysis due to task decoupling. On the other hand, some researches study the (online) SGD training dynamics and sample complexity of learning a two-layer neural network, e.g., [ 91 , 92 ]. However, relatively little theory is known about how these three factors—model size, data size, and training steps—jointly determine scaling behavior in deep architectures. In this paper, we aim to address this gap.

Definition 1 (Ermergent Intelligence) . If the limit lim  

> N,P,K →∞

E(N, P, K ) exists in any sense, we say that the foundation model M = {fW , D, A} displays intelligent emergenence, and E(∞, ∞, ∞) = lim

E(N, P, K ) is the emergent intelligence (performance) exhibited by M.

Our key insight is that intelligence emerges as a transition from finite to effec-tively infinite knowledge, architecture and training steps, and we recast ermergent intelligence as the existence of the limit lim N,P,K →∞ E(N, P, K ). Then note that the existence of the limit implies the usability of foundation models regarding the con-cerned tasks. In fact, a trained foundation model is usable only when N is sufficiently large (indicating extensive knowledge acquisition), P is sufficiently large (indicating a large scale of model parameters), and T is sufficiently large (indicating sufficiently well-trained for the models), the model can exhibit stable learning behavior, hopefully, the expected favorable performance. This is characterized exactly by the existence of limit lim N,P,K →∞ E(N, P, K ), or by definition, for any ε > 0, and for sufficient large

Especially, when the range of E(N, P, K ) is discrete or discontinous, the performance of the foundation model might exhibit “abrupt performance improvement”, i.e., emergent abilities.

The central difficulty is whether a neural network with infinite depth defines a stable architecture. The key challenge lies in analyzing the infinite-depth limit, namely, whether infinite-depth neural networks constitute a stable architecture. Besides, the sample error quantifies the deviation between the solution learned from finite data and the infinite-data limit associated with 11 the limit architecture. Existing studies primarily analyze the estimation error of finite-parameter models, whereas the infinite-parameter regime remains largely unexplored [100 , 101 ]. In this work, we provide a theoretical analysis of the architecture error and the sample error in Sections 7.2 and 7.3, respectively. Compared with classical machine learning theory, the novel theories provide convergence analysis for infinitely deep networks, along with corresponding generalization bounds.

Let K denote the total number of layers in a given foundation model. First, we select the k-th layer at specific relative depths, where k represents 5%, 20%, 40%, 60% and 80% of the total network depth (i.e., k ∈ { 0.05 K, 0.2K, 0.4K, 0.6K, 0.8K}). During a standard forward pass, we extract the hidden state input to the k-th layer, denoting it as ak.Subsequently, we bypass the model’s sequential computational graph by treating the extracted intermediate representation ak as an independent input. Specifically, we feed ak separately into each individual layer of the model (from layer 1 to K), which produces a set of corresponding single-layer outputs denoted by {oki }Ki=1 . To quantify the functional transformation applied by each layer, we compute both the Euclidean distance and the cosine similarity between the injected input ak and the corresponding output oki of the i-th layer 1. Formally, the Euclidean distance is defined as 

dki = ∥ak − oki ∥2, i = 1 , 2, · · · , K, 

and the cosine similarity is given by 

ski = ⟨ak, o ki ⟩∥ak∥2 ∥oki ∥2

, i = 1 , 2, · · · , K. 

We compute the mean and variance of dki and ski across all samples in the dataset. The smaller the distance (i.e., the higher the similarity), the more the layer tends to behave like a projection operator. 

Experimental results: Figures 4-13 show the computed metrics (Euclidean/cosine distance) of different layers for different foundation models. In each figure, rows correspond to different task groups (QA=SQuAD, Math=GSM8K, Summ=DialogSum, and MT=FairTranslate) and columns correspond to selected layer indices k. As evidently shown in these figures, the open-source foundation models exhibit condensing property. Specifically, when the selected intermediate representation ak is extracted from deeper layers (e.g., k = 0 .5K or 0 .9K), the distance between ak and the outputs of the subsequent layers {oki } tends to approach zero (indicating extremely high similarity). This observation indicates that, beyond a certain depth, the representations across subsequent network layers undergo almost no substantive change. In other words, the first k layers can be viewed as forming an approximate “fixed point” for the remaining layers, exhibiting similar functional behavior as the potential limit architecture. The remaining layers can be viewed as approximating the projection operator. This further confirms the condensing property among existing well-performing foundation models, which suggests the condensing property as a core condition for the existence of ermergent intelligence, as demonstrated in Section 6. ## 10 Conclusion 

In this work, we have proposed a rigorous mathematical framework for understanding ermergent intelligence and scaling law from a limit perspective. We formulate a foudation model as the composition of stacked basic functional blocks, and introduce the limit architecture to embody the infinite-parameters limit of the foundation models, corresponding to an infinite-dimensional system. Our theory reveals that ermergent intelligence originates from the existence of the limit architecture, and that emergent abilities correspond precisely to the learning behavior of this limit architecture. We further introduce the Lip constant of nonlinear operators to precisely quantify properties of the basic blocks, and prove that existence of the limit architecture is guaranteed if and only if there exists a constant K > 0 such that Lip (Ti) ≤ 1, ∀i ≥ K, and the sequence {Ti} satisfies a condensing property by utilizing theory of Lipschitz dual operator and spectral theory of compact operators. Besides, we derive scaling laws showing that the performance function of foundation models depends on data size, model size and training steps, following the scaling βK + Lip (T )P + N 12 . Our theoretical analysis reveals that emergent intelligence is governed by training steps, data scale, and model architecture, with the properties of basic building blocks playing a central role. We show that the critical condition Lip (T ) = 1 aligns with prior findings [ 1, 2 ], providing theoretical support. Moreover, although emergent intelligence originates from an infinite-dimensional system, it can be effectively realized via finite-dimensional architectures, offering a theoretical explanation for empirical observations [3]. Empirically, we demonstrate that the Lip constant takes a crucial role in evaluating the usability of foundation models, and could help assess architectural stability. We further substantiate the condensing property in state-of-the-art open-source foundation models. Our analysis assumes that foundation models are constructed by stacking basic functional blocks. An interesting extension is to consider more general composition mechanisms, such as residual connections and mixture-of-experts architectures, and to derive corresponding conditions for the existence of a limit architecture. Another promising direction is to characterize the precise form of the limit architecture and leverage its properties to provide theoretical guidance for the architectural design of foundation models. Finally, our limit theory suggests that foundation models may achieve benign overfitting by enforcing the existence of a limit architecture.
